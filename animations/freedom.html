Advanced Animation project

Up until now you have been learning the basics of how to make animation, everything from lighting to rendering

Now, it's time to combine all of your skills and explore.



Your assignment is simple.

You will create a 30 second to 1 min animation on the subject of your choosing.

Ready to create?!


Brainstorming

Where the creativity happens

Take some time and start thinking about subject material.

For a thirty second to one minute animation, you need to have a plan. The key to a successful, dynamic action animation is a thoroughly carved out plan.

The way to do this is to brainstorm your way out.

Do not skip this step.

Not sketching out a storyboard will make your job harder. The storyboard is akin to a blueprint for an architect. You wouldn't hire an architect without a plan.

Start storyboarding

Grab a pencil and paper and make a grid similar to below or start sketching in GIMP using this template

For a thirty second to one minute animation, you should aim to have at least a frame a second.

These drawings do not need to be overly complex. They can literally be one stick figure interacting with another.

Asset Store

If you are creating your own assets, skip this section.

Using Unity as your animator you have an advantage because your subject material is just a click away in the asset store.

Simply find your models online.

Asset creation

If you are using the Unity asset store for your subjects, skip this section.

One of the biggest reasons why animation is so time consuming, is due to asset creation.

Creating your own assets can be time consuming, but the benefits are overwhelming

There is nothing quite as inspiring as working with your own characters. The passion of using your own characters and personalized drawings correlates into the story, it can make a basic animation have a lot of character and life.

Start sketching

Whether on paper or in GIMP, asset creation by drawing can be frustrating but incredibly rewarding.

This is when your storyboarding pages will worth their weight in gold to help you create your assets.

Building

Start to build out your animation keyframe by keyframe.

This is when the magic happens.

This is when all the work you put in building your storyboards, creating and working with your assets will really help in the end.

If you get stuck or are short on time, repeat the storyboard step. Play the animation constantly and work with the pace.

Part of a 30sec to 1min animation is a dynamic story, that's concise, clear, to the point but has a great story and build up.

Critique

The critique is a fundamental part of the animation process. This helps you discuss ideas out with your peers and instructors. Be prepared for criticism. While it is hard to hear that your animation didn't cut the mustard, listen to your responses and push your animation to be even better


It's time to get feedback

Choose two fellow peers. Ask them for honest feedback on your animation. Ask them what they like. What they don't like. Take notes, and ask what they would change.

Reach out to an instructor

Understandably, your fellow peers might not be working on the same course work you are. They might not know anything about Animation or storyboarding, but this is where your instructor can help

Ask one of your instructors to critique your animation. Set up a time to meet and get back together to chat about your piece. Somtimes during class, is not the best time to critique. You will get the best advice by allowing the instructor to mull on it and give you a solid review of your work.

Final Step: Revise

After your review, you have changes to make.

Every animation has revisions and changes. No one gets it right the first time, not even the strongest Animators. Critiques and changes to work are normal. Clients often see their idea fleshed out, and it's not always the first draft.

Critiques are a common occurance for the professional Animator, and often saught after by freelancers.
