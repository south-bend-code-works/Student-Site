Unity Student Guide 1 - Worldbuilding with Terrain: The Basics

In this lesson you will:
  1. Explore WorldBuilding
  2. See real world examples of successful games

What is Worldbuilding?

You actually know the answer without knowing the term. Worldbuilding is a term that creators use to create a living and breathing story. Every little detail of the envirment surrounding you is part of worldbuilding because it's part of the overall arcing story that the game builders want you to experience.

Worldbuilding can be a fast process or a slow one, it's the ability to construct an imaginary world, and a whole fictional universe.

Some games are light on the worldbuilding, think Candy Crush. Your whole environment revolves around one type of food.

Other games are intense in the worldbuilding.

Think the Legend of Zelda games or Blizzard games. Both are great examples of creating stories within a brand new universe that people want to play and experience.

You can go as deep as you want with the story. Several planets full of intense dialog and lore, or a one sided story where your character wakes up with amnesia.

It's your world.

Now let's start brainstorming

Grab some paper and a pen

This is akin to wireframing, but with some anthropology thrown in there.

Start thinking about Geography. Is there water? Dynamic landcapes? Is this a desert or a fertile land? Is it a completely foreign world where grass is purple and the trees are blue?

Start thinking about Cultures. Our world has thousands of cultures, draw on some that you know or research. Think power structure, governments, religions, arts or entertainment. Even stuble hints make a game richer and more playable.

Start thinking about History. Are there ruins? Traumatic events like wars, famines, or a meteor? These impact the terrain.

Start thinking about Magic or Technology. Is your world magical? Is it something spoken in whispers and practicers condemed? Can magic be defeated or destroyed? Does Tech rule the world?

Flesh some of these ideas out on a piece of paper.

Mind Map

Mind maps are good because they allow you to build backstory about a character, transporting them from a flat npc (non-player character) to a multi-dimensional character.

All of this is important because of continuity

Don't you hate it when you are watching a movie and all of a sudden the main character's shirt changes from white to blue with no explaination? Or the Medieval warrior with a wristwatch for only part of the battle?

Continuity can make or break your game. When you break it, the player will either giggle at the mistake and think you are inexperienced, but do it enough and it breaks the experience. Continuity is something to be mindful of. Be careful, create strong notes, and consult them often, and your game will be off to a great start.

Making the Terrain

Let's jump into Unity

Create a new game with instructions from Setup

Click Create in the Hierarchy on the left-hand side, press 3D Object → Terrain

Now you have your first world. Let's jazz it up!

Adding Textures

Click the Terrain in the Hierarchy (right) to open it in the Inspector (left).

Click the dropdown menu that says Create Neighbor Terrains, and select Paint Textures

Find the texture you want to be the default for the terrain in the project window: Scroll through the options, or search for "grass”, “rock”, or “mud”. Click the one you want, then press Enter.

For each Terrain layer you create, use one texture. This isn't a limit on your worldbuilding. To add more textures, hit the Edit Terrain Layers → Create Layer button again and add another in the same way.

Painting the Terrain

You can paint the terrain with the mouse in the Scene view.

Brushes: the shape of the brush you're painting with.

Terrain Layers: click the one you want to paint with.

Brush size: the size of the brush.

Opacity: the transparency of the texture. You can layer textures with different opacities.

Not finding the terrain layers you want? Look for more terrains in the Asset Store

Changing the Height of the Terrain

Click the dropdown menu that says Paint Texture, and select Raise or Lower Terrain.

The Raise/Lower terrain tool allows you to change the shape of the terrain. You can make mountains, valleys, hills, etc. Paint with it just like the texture painting. You can adjust the brush, brush size and opacity just like the other tool.

Click: raise the terrain.

Shift + Click: lower the terrain.

Opacity: how much you change the size. Keep this low for small adjustments or high for big changes in the landscape.

Worldbuilding Tip:

Use the terrain to your story building advantage. Create a path for the character to navigate through the moutains by building steep terrain. Guide your player so that later you can put treasures or bosses in areas they are sure to run into.

You can also use the Set Height option in the drop down to set the height of your terrain to a specific value.

The Smooth Height option can be used to smooth out jagged edges and points.
Worldbuilding Tip:

Mountains are a dynamic story tool. Is there a crater where your character emerges from after crash landing on your world? Is there an ominous ring of mountains around the terrain you're in? Is it keeping your character in or the world outside from coming in?
