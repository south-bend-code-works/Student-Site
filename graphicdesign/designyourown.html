Create your own typeface

Typefaces are easy to find on the internet. There are many websites to choose from like www.dafont.com, www.fontsquirrel.com, fonts.google.com

But sometimes, none of those typefaces or fonts work. This is when you have to get creative. Designing your own font is a fun and easy way to have a copyright free typeface, that you won't have to pay for.

You can make it as fun as cooky as you are, or as dull and drab as a bingo social.

The possiblities are endless.

There are designers that have dedicated themselves to font and typeface design. This lesson is something you can do multiple times and have many different possiblities.

You can never have too many fonts and typefaces.

But how do you create and design a winning font?

Let's think back to our Bad fonts lesson:

Keep in mind of Kerning. That space between letters can add up. While you can fix the kerning manually, it is time consuming and is a hallmark of a bad typeface.

Don't forget to keep it Legibile. If it's illegible, people have to make a huge effort to read what you're trying to say. It's easy to lose interest along the way, even if it's fancy. Cursive is a great example. When you're creating a font, think about adding style but without sacrificing legiblity.


Many font designers start with another font that's simple and then add flair and style.

Let's get started!

Brainstorm

Start brainstorming ideas. Get out a pen and paper. Do some research.

Do you want a very elegant typeface? Something with a bit of a kick?

What kind of message are you trying to convey?

Do you want a serif? A sans serif?


Open GIMP

Start with a bland typeface

Use the text tool and start with Arial, Times New Roman, or Calibri. These are typefaces that can give a good foundation to a font. From there, you can start to add or take away to individual letters, making them your own.

Don't want to start out adding or deleting to an existing font?

While this method will give you the most freedom, it is the hardest. Designing a typeface front scratch can be great for handwritten fonts, or fonts that are extremely unique.

Get that paintbrush out and practice your caligraphy.

Go wild

Designing fonts and typefaces is a lot of fun. Now get creative and start designing!

Review

The critique is a fundamental part of the design process. This helps you discuss ideas out with your peers and instructors. Be prepared for criticism. While it is hard to hear that your design didn't cut the mustard, listen to your responses and push your design to be even better

It's time to get feedback

Choose two fellow peers. Ask them for honest feedback on your logo. Ask them what they like. What they don't like. Take notes, and ask what they would change.

Reach out to an instructor

Understandably, your fellow peers might not be working on the same course work you are. They might not know anything about Graphic Design, but this is where your instructor can help

Ask one of your instructors to critique your design. Set up a time to meet and get back together to chat about your logo. Somtimes during class, is not the best time to critique. You will get the best advice by allowing the instructor to mull on it and give you a solid review of your work.

Final Step: Revise

After your review, you have changes to make.

Every design has revisions and changes. No one gets it right the first time, not even the strongest Graphic Designers. Critiques and changes to a design are normal. Clients often see their idea fleshed out, and it's not always the first draft.

Critiques are a common occurance for the professional Graphic Designer, and often saught after by freelancers.
