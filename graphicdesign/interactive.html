Interactive Print Media

QR Codes are a fundamental way to communicate a link fast and easy to a consumer.

They are generated by putting a link into a third party website, like Google, and an image is generated that allows users to use an app to find your link.

They are extremely useful, and allow an easy way for a user to go to a link that can otherwise be way too time consuming to type.


The problem is that they can look bulky, clunky, and oddly slapped on there with no account for design. This is your chance as a graphic designer to change how a QR code is presented and incorporated it into your design.

Food Drive

Your local University is holding a Spring food drive and heard that you have been studying Graphic Design and would like a student to design it.

The event is sponsored by the History Club

It will be held the week of April 1st-5th, and drop off times are from 9-9p at the library.

They have more information on what kind of food they are looking for at their website,

mylocaluniversity.org/hunger-being-solved-one-homework-assignment-at-a-time/spring/drive

(use this for your QR code)

Your assignment is to create a 16x20" poster of the event, working the QR code into the design as a fun and quirky, way that is still obvious to the public that it is a QR code

Use Google or another QR code generator to create a code.

One of the main aspects of coding is testing your work, so don't forget to test the QR code with your or someone else's phone.

Open GIMP

Create a 16x20" poster in GIMP

Either paste the QR code in as an image, or import it

Brainstorming

Start thinking of how to incorporate the QR code into the design

Are there any places that make sense for the code to go?

How can you make the code stand out without blending into the background of the poster?

Review

The critique is a fundamental part of the design process. This helps you discuss ideas out with your peers and instructors. Be prepared for criticism. While it is hard to hear that your design didn't cut the mustard, listen to your responses and push your design to be even better


It's time to get feedback

Choose two fellow peers. Ask them for honest feedback on your logo. Ask them what they like. What they don't like. Take notes, and ask what they would change.

Reach out to an instructor

Understandably, your fellow peers might not be working on the same course work you are. They might not know anything about Graphic Design, but this is where your instructor can help

Ask one of your instructors to critique your design. Set up a time to meet and get back together to chat about your logo. Somtimes during class, is not the best time to critique. You will get the best advice by allowing the instructor to mull on it and give you a solid review of your work.

Final Step: Revise

After your review, you have changes to make.

Every design has revisions and changes. No one gets it right the first time, not even the strongest Graphic Designers. Critiques and changes to a design are normal. Clients often see their idea fleshed out, and it's not always the first draft.

Critiques are a common occurance for the professional Graphic Designer, and often saught after by freelancers.
