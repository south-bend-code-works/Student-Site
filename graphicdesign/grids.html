What is a grid?

In this lesson you will:
  1. Learn about wireframes
  2. Create a wireframe website

In design, a grid is a system for organizing layout. The layouts could be for print (like a book, magazine, or poster), or for screen (like a webpage, app, or other user interface). There are a lot of different types of grid, and they all serve different purposes. Here are some of the main examples:

Baseline grid

Equally spaced horizontal lines that determine where text will sit, it's a grid is a sheet of ruled paper.

Column grid

Newspapers and magazines use column grids extensively.

Modular grid

Kind of an extension of the column grid, a modular grid involves taking a column grid and adding rows to it

Manuscript grid

Classic, “traditional” books use a manuscript grid.

Pixel grid

Digital screens are made up of a microscopic grid of millions of pixels, and sometimes designers get in close to edit images pixel-by-pixel.

Hierarchical grid

This refers to any irregular grid that accommodates specific content needs.


This all leads us to one question, why are grids important?

Grids are a great place to start. Whether you're stuck on design, or simply want a clean idea, grids are a great starting point.

Wireframes

A website wireframe, also known as a page schematic or screen blueprint, is a visual guide that represents the skeletal framework of a website. Wireframes are created for the purpose of arranging elements to best accomplish a particular purpose.

Practice with wireframing

You are designing a website for a neighborhood dog walker. They don't have pictures of their clients yet, but want a menu that has services, testimonials, a blog, and an FAQ. Use GIMP to build a simple wireframe. Add sample text such as lorem ipsum and headers, leaving striked out boxes for sample pictures

Open GIMP

Start to put in vital details of the web page such as the navigation bar and images

Use coolors.co/app to pick your colour schemes.

Don't go too overboard with colors. Wireframing is a preliminary step before designing a website. Wireframes are great to show to potential clients, but they are meant to be fast rough drafts.

Design Review + Critique

The critique is a fundamental part of the design process. This helps you discuss ideas out with your peers and instructors. Be prepared for criticism.

It's time to get feedback!

Peer Feedback

Choose two peers. Ask them for honest feedback on your poster. Ask them what they like. What they don't like. Take notes, and ask what they would change.
Instructor Feedback

Ask one of your instructors to critique your design. Set up a time to meet and get back together to chat about your poster. Ask them what they like. What they don't like. Take notes, and ask what they would change.
Revise

Every design has revisions and changes. No one gets it right the first time.
